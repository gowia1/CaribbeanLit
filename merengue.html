Merengue

History

Merengue began in the Dominican Republic in the late nineteenth century and became one of the most important musical and dance traditions in the country. According to the source, it developed from a blend of European musical styles such as contradanza, waltz, and polka with African rhythms introduced by enslaved people.

In its early years, merengue used simple instruments such as the accordion and güira, and rural communities played a major role in preserving and spreading the genre. As the style continued to grow, it became more widely recognized throughout the Dominican Republic.

From the 1930s to the 1960s, merengue entered a golden age and became a national symbol of Dominican culture. Later, it spread internationally, especially to the United States, where Dominican communities helped keep the genre alive and popular.

Outside Influences

Merengue clearly reflects cultural mixing. European dance forms shaped part of its structure, while African rhythms helped create its energy and movement. In later years, merengue also adapted by blending with newer styles such as pop and hip-hop.

Influence on Caribbean Music

Merengue is important to the development of Caribbean music because it shows how genres can remain tied to national identity while still changing over time. Its spread to international audiences also shows how Caribbean music moves beyond the region and continues to evolve.

Influential Artists

Johnny Ventura and Wilfrido Vargas are two influential artists connected to merengue. The source specifically highlights Johnny Ventura’s role in helping modernize and popularize the genre.
